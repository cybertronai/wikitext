3 · What the executed runs actually tell us

3.1 · Pure paradigm-A loses on representation, not on solver

Three closed-form paradigm-A submissions krr_ngram / rff_ridge_v1 / poly_tensorsketch_ridge all landed at 0.30 – 0.37 val acc at 100 – 2,565 J. The energies are remarkable (≈100× cheaper than baseline), but accuracy bottoms out far below 0.70 regardless of which fixed feature space is used (n-grams / RFF / polynomial tensor sketch). The solver isn’t the problem — the inability to learn task-adapted features is. This empirically confirms the finding_kernel_two_paradigms.md prediction: paradigm A cannot clear the floor on its own.

3.2 · The naive paradigm-A + paradigm-B hybrid also loses

nystrom_krr_hybrid (exp 07) trained a 4-layer encoder for ~140 s and then closed-form solved a Nyström KRR readout. Result: 32 kJ / 0.300 val acc — worse than the encoder + linear head of exp 02 (0.588) at half the energy. The diagnosis is in the spec’s pre-registered Failure Modes:

“Phase 1 encoder collapses without an output head being trained jointly: train phase 1 with an output head (standard LM loss), then discard the head.”

That's likely what happened — the embedding geometry the encoder learned wasn’t aligned to the next-byte target the way the joint output head would align it. Next step: rerun with the discarded-head protocol the spec already specifies before adjudicating deep kernel learning on this benchmark.

3.3 · Paradigm B works — when training fits in the budget

Two pure paradigm-B submissions ran:

  • performer_favor (exp 03): hit the 300 s train cap — FAVOR+ random-feature attention has a higher per-step constant factor than softmax attention at T = 1024, where the T² attention matrix that FAVOR+ avoids isn't yet the bottleneck. The "free linear-attention" framing assumes long context. Predicted by the memory-movement analysis in the spec (lower HBM only pays at long T), but observed only now.
  • hopfield_layer (exp 11): a single attention-over-frozen-memory layer added to a smaller transformer. Trained in 184 s (well under cap), 40,158 J / 0.7293 val acc → passes the floor and beats baseline by 22 %. This is the one win in the portfolio so far.

3.4 · The Hopfield result is the empirical confirmation of the "kernel-as-component" thesis

The Hopfield layer is mathematically softmax-attention over a fixed external memory bank (Ramsauer et al. 2020). It is the cleanest possible test of kernel density retrieval as a model component, sitting inside an otherwise standard transformer. The fact that this works while the pure kernel-machine paradigm-A submissions fail, and the deep-kernel hybrid fails too, is consistent with the strongest interpretation of the survey’s pre-registered framing: kernels help language modeling when they are a building block, not a replacement.

The "structural ceiling" of the benchmark (byte-by-byte predict() at eval prevents cross-byte parallelism) was felt by all five runs equally; it didn’t single any kernel approach out. Where the kernel-methods family does consume parallelism differently from the baseline is at training time:

  • FAVOR+ and DeltaNet trade matmul fan-out for either random-feature arithmetic (FAVOR+) or chunkwise sequential dependency (DeltaNet). Both pay a per-step constant-factor tax that only amortizes at longer T than this benchmark uses.
  • Hopfield retrieval is the opposite: it’s pure additional attention matmuls against a frozen memory bank — fully parallel, tensor-core friendly, no recurrence, no random features. Cheapest parallelism path of any kernel method tested, and (not coincidentally) the only one that wins.

5 · What this means for the remaining seven experiments

Promote
Exp 11 follow-ups: memory-bank size ablation, learned-memory vs. frozen-memory A/B, two-Hopfield-layers variant. These are not yet in the portfolio but are the obvious extension of the only working direction.
Run next, as written
Exp 06 (Nyströmformer) — non-random-feature paradigm-B; sidesteps the per-step-cost failure mode of FAVOR+. Exp 04 (linear-tx elu+1) — cheapest paradigm-B variant; if it also blows the budget like FAVOR+, the budget itself (not the random features) is the issue.
Re-spec before running
Exp 07 should be re-run with the train-with-head-then-discard protocol the spec lists under Failure Modes (the current implementation likely skipped that step).
Keep but de-prioritize
Exp 05 (DeltaNet) — high effort (~200 LoC hand-roll), and the FAVOR+ result suggests linear-attention variants pay a per-step tax that doesn’t amortize at T=1024. Exp 08 (Parzen-KDE) — pure paradigm A; the three executed paradigm-A submissions all bottom out at ≈0.36. Unlikely to clear 0.50 without a learned encoder, in which case it’s closer to exp 11 anyway.
Cross-pollination still worth a shot
Exp 10 (LWTA × arc-cosine head): two cheap gradient-free mechanisms composed; small absolute cost; high information value either way.

6 · Open questions surfaced by the executed runs

  • Why did performer_favor miss 300 s while hopfield_layer hit 184 s? Both replace/augment attention; the Hopfield extra layer is "another attention block" while FAVOR+ is "different attention". The per-step cost ordering FAVOR+ > softmax > (softmax + Hopfield-against-4k-memory) deserves a profiled comparison.
  • What's the smallest memory bank that still beats baseline? The Hopfield spec used M = 4096; if M = 1024 still passes, the energy headroom is even larger.
  • Does the Hopfield win survive learned memory? Currently the bank is frozen patterns from training data. If learned-memory equals or beats frozen, this collapses into "yet another transformer layer with extra tied weights" and the kernel-density framing is decorative.
  • Did nystrom_krr_hybrid actually follow the train-with-head-then-discard protocol from the spec? Worth checking the submission code before treating 0.300 as a refutation of deep kernel learning.
