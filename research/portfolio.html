Energy-efficient character-language-model research

A portfolio of investigations into training next-character predictors at minimum energy on WikiText-103.

Working tree of cybertronai/wikitext on branch gradfree-methods, snapshot 2026-05-18.

The research question. Can a learning algorithm reach a respectable accuracy threshold on a non-toy language task while spending materially less energy than a modern gradient-descent-trained transformer?

The benchmark. WikiText-103, character-level prediction. Submissions must reach at least 0.70 character-prediction accuracy on the validation set, training in under 300 seconds on a single NVIDIA A100-80GB. They are ranked by training energy in joules, measured directly via NVML and idle-subtracted.

Active investigations

Pointer-Sentinel mixture LM Active submissions/pointer_sentinel/

What it is, in plain terms

A char-level adaptation of the Merity et al. (2017) Pointer-Sentinel Mixture LM. A small transformer backbone (d=256, L=4) produces a query at each position. That query is dotted against the embeddings of the last W=1,024 buffered characters plus one learnable sentinel key. A softmax over the (W+1) scores yields a mixture gate g — the softmax mass on the sentinel — and a copy distribution P_ptr over the buffer. The final next-byte distribution is g·P_vocab + (1−g)·P_ptr.

Why it has traction here

Long-form text is full of repeated character substrings — named entities, repeated phrases, citations, source-code identifiers. A literal copy from the recent buffer covers those at the cost of one attention dot product over a small window; the parametric backbone only has to model the truly novel positions. The mechanism is structurally orthogonal to LWTA's "make the transformer cheaper" approach: it shrinks the parametric model by offloading work onto a non-parametric copy distribution.

Result

0.6712 val char-acc at 20,348 J in 158 s. Below the 0.70 floor by 0.029, but at ~40% of the baseline's energy. This is the highest accuracy any sub-floor submission reaches, and the highest energy efficiency of any non-trivial neural method on the board.

Open

Widening the backbone, lengthening the buffer past 1,024, or both. 0.029 is a small gap on a configuration that ran first-try; the simplest scale-up dispatch is the obvious next experiment, and is the cheapest plausible non-LWTA pass on the board.

LWTA (Local Winner-Take-All) drop-in Active submissions/lwta_k4/, lwta_k2/

What it is, in plain terms

LWTA is a 2013 idea from Srivastava, Masci, Kazerounian, Gomez and Schmidhuber ("Compete to Compute"). Inside each MLP block of a transformer, you partition the hidden units into small groups of k. For each group, only the unit with the highest pre-activation passes its value forward — the rest are zeroed. Structurally, only 1/k of the MLP forward signal propagates, and per token only 1/k of the MLP rows receive a gradient.

Energy story

The original paper framed LWTA as a regularizer, not an efficiency play. The potential energy reduction has two compounding parts, only one of which the current submissions realize:

  • Realized: backward through the LWTA mask zeroes gradients on losers, so the dense matmuls in the backward pass operate on sparse inputs/outputs. Combined with a cheaper activation (argmax + scatter vs GELU's erf), the LWTA blocks run ~10% faster per step than the baseline at identical n_steps.
  • Unrealized: the post-activation matmul is structurally sparse — a fused Triton kernel that skips the zero columns would extract another ~k× on that one operation, projected to compound to ~30% total energy reduction at k=4.

The reduction is not from reduced optimizer-state traffic: Muon iterates every parameter with a non-None gradient, and after a dense-mask backward every weight tensor has one. Across a training batch all rows of fc see some token's gradient.

Result

k=4 beats k=2 by 0.009 accuracy at the same energy: the higher-sparsity variant performs slightly better, consistent with LWTA's classical regularization story. Neither submission shows winner-collapse (no single unit dominating its group), which was the failure mode flagged in the spec.

Open

(a) A fused Triton kernel for the LWTA + W₂ matmul step, to realize the unrealized half of the energy story. (b) A k=8 datapoint to see whether the regularization-vs-capacity trade keeps favouring sparsity.

PPM (Prediction by Partial Matching) — C extension follow-up Active research/ppm-c-extension/, submissions/ppm_c/

What it is, in plain terms

PPM is the simplest learning algorithm on this board: it counts. For every short character sequence it has seen, it remembers what came next and how often. To predict, it looks at the last few characters and picks the most frequent continuation. There is no gradient descent, no neural network, no GPU required — just a tree of counters that grows as text streams in. It is a 1984 algorithm that the data compression community has used for decades.

The two PPM submissions

A pure-Python implementation reached 0.6300 / 633 J having seen only ~2% of the training data in the 300 s budget — throughput-limited. A C-extension reimplementation (ppm_core.c) ingests all 541 M training bytes in 108 s, building a depth-6 context tree with 10.2 M nodes and 103 M context-symbol entries, and reaches 0.6525 / 735 J. A 50× increase in training data moved accuracy by only +0.022 — PPM's character-level learning curve flattens past the first few percent of WikiText.

What that means

The algorithmic ceiling, not implementation throughput, is the binding constraint. Scaling up PPM further is not the path forward. The structural next move is spec-7: PPM plus a small neural residual head over its predictions, which only needs to add 0.05 char-acc to clear the floor — a much easier ask than what residual learning faced over the pure-Python PPM.

As a reference point

At 735 J, PPM-C is by some margin the lowest-energy submission that produces non-trivial accuracy (the next non-PPM run, ES p1, is 1,938 J / 0.19; the only lower-energy run is a 3-second random projection at 70 J / 0.22). PPM-C does not pass the floor, but it serves as a tight count-based anchor for any future hybrid that uses count-based features as input.

Open

The spec-7 hybrid: a small neural residual head that takes PPM-C's predicted distribution as input and learns the residual. Energy budget for the residual head is what's left of the 51 kJ baseline after PPM-C's 735 J.

Forward-Forward — exhaustive investigation Active — Phase 2 of 8 research/forward-forward-deep/

What it is, in plain terms

Forward-Forward is a 2022 proposal by Geoffrey Hinton: train a neural network without ever computing gradients backward through layers. Each layer learns to make its own activation pattern "energetic" for real data and "inert" for synthetic negative data — a per-layer contrastive rule. The promise is a learning algorithm that fits the way biological neurons work, with constant memory per layer and no backward sweep.

Why investigate it deeply

The 2022 paper benchmarked Forward-Forward on small image classification tasks and reported accuracies a few points below backpropagation. The literature since has produced roughly 20 named variants. Almost none have been tested on language data. If a Forward-Forward variant can clear the 0.70 floor here, it would be the first credible large-scale demonstration of layer-local learning on a real text task.

What the Phase-2 sweep found

Five Phase-2 runs answered "what is the rate-limiter?":

  • Bigram-aware input encoding lifted accuracy by 0.12 (from 0.28 to 0.40) — the biggest single improvement of any variant tested. The input encoding, not the learning rule, is the binding constraint.
  • Widening from 384 to 1024 units gave another 0.06 lift.
  • Pure random projection (no learning at all) hit 0.22 — the FF rule itself adds only ~0.06 over the random baseline.
  • Longer context (K=24 → K=64) hurt accuracy by 0.07 — the network cannot use distant context with this depth and encoding.

Open

Phase 3 (six contrastive-rule variants screened in parallel) and Phases 4–7 (encoder, depth, target, and goodness-function sweeps). The investigation will produce one of two outcomes: a Forward-Forward variant clears 0.70, or a structured discard. Both are valuable given how little of the post-2022 FF literature has been tested on language.

Alternative architectures — Mamba, Hyena, Schmidhuber chunker Active submissions/mamba/, hyena/, chunker/; research/catalog/new_directions/chunker_d1/

What this group is

Three architectures that swap or augment standard self-attention with sub-quadratic mixers, all submitted as drop-in replacements for the baseline backbone:

  • Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) — a selective state-space model with input-dependent recurrent transitions and a 1-D conv ring buffer. Training uses a parallel scan; inference is O(d_state) per byte.
  • Hyena (Poli et al., 2023) — element-wise gates around implicit long convolutions (filters parameterised by a small MLP from position → tap weight), computed causally via FFT.
  • Schmidhuber chunker (Schmidhuber 1992) — a small automatizer network L predicts every byte; bytes with L-confidence below threshold τ are flagged "surprises" and routed to a larger chunker network H, which only runs on the surprise stream.

What the runs show

All three train inside the 300 s budget. None complete the 60,000-character byte-by-byte evaluation in the remaining harness wall-clock:

  • Mamba: training 72 kJ in 285 s; eval ran at 108 char/s through pure-PyTorch parallel scan, reaching 0.5654 at 52% through the validation split before being killed. The partial trajectory is essentially flat in the 0.56–0.58 band, suggesting the architecture is viable here and only the inference substrate is in the way.
  • Hyena: training 55 kJ in 281 s; eval ran at ~60 char/s and reached 0.2027 partial. The decode path re-runs an FFT over the entire prefix at each byte, so eval cost grows linearly with position.
  • Chunker: a Phase-0 diagnostic (chunker_d1) trained the L network alone and measured the surprise-rate distribution — p_s(τ=0.1) = 0.27, which clears the spec's go criterion. The full L+H run trained at 12.6 kJ in 222 s, eval reached 0.3001 at 80% before being killed.

Why these are grouped

They share a failure mode that is structurally distinct from the algorithmic DQs elsewhere on the board. Training works; inference substrate is what disqualifies. Anything whose pure-PyTorch decode is slower than ~200 char/s trips the harness wall before scoring 60,000 characters.

Open

For Mamba: the upstream mamba-ssm CUDA scan kernel, or a windowed-eval mode that does decode in fixed-size chunks instead of byte-by-byte. Either resolves the eval wall directly; Mamba's partial trajectory makes it the most likely of the three to produce a competitive final number.

For Hyena: a streaming-FFT or causal-conv-cache decode path. Higher effort than Mamba; lower-priority given the lower partial accuracy.

For the chunker: a KV-cache decoding path on both L and H. The D1 diagnostic confirms the surprise rate is in-spec, so the architecture is worth a second attempt once the cached decode is in.

Survey-only investigations

Manning bibliography survey Survey-only research/manning-bibliography/

What this is

A read-through of roughly 210 publications from Christopher Manning's Stanford bibliography (1991–2025), filtering for methods that could plausibly be ported to the WikiText energy benchmark.

Top finding

Word-level prediction with character emission is the highest-prior unexplored direction. A 5-gram modified Kneser-Ney model over WikiText words emits the characters of its predicted word deterministically, paying full prediction cost only at word boundaries. This collapses 60,000 character predictions into ~12,000 word predictions; a moderately heavy word model comes in well under the baseline's 51 kJ.

Why this is "survey-only" rather than active

No experiments have been run from this list yet. The Kneser-Ney word model is the recommended first pick — lowest engineering cost, highest probability of clearing 0.70.

Status

Markdown report only. Next action is to pick one candidate and promote it to an active investigation.

Neural Bucket Brigade (NBB) diagnostic Closed-discard — with novel finding research/nbb-bigram-diagnostic/

NBB (Schmidhuber 1989) is a fully gradient-free local learning rule that propagates "substance" through a network like a reward signal. Tested as a bigram next-byte predictor, no configuration could reach even the unigram floor. The cause is structural, not implementational: an analytical derivation shows that NBB's multiplicative-update dynamics cannot have a stable equilibrium when output probabilities vary by token — either rare-token connections dissipate to zero or common-token connections blow up. This appears to be a novel finding (no published paper documents it). Implication: any other multiplicative-update local rule deserves the same sanity check before investing engineering.

Reproducibility & methodology

Hardware

All training and energy measurements on Modal-provisioned NVIDIA A100-80GB (PCIe for baseline, LWTA, PPM-C and modded-nanogpt; SXM4 for the Forward-Forward Phase-2 batch). Idle power differs by ~30%; energy measurements are idle-subtracted.

Energy measurement

Direct NVML readings from the device's on-chip energy counter, sampled at the start and end of training, then idle-subtracted. Energy includes only the GPU portion of training.

Evaluation harness

The runner calls the submission's predict() method strictly before its observe() method for each position, so future-peeking is structurally impossible. Validation accuracy is computed on the first 60,000 characters of the official WikiText-103 validation split via greedy argmax.

Code & data

All submission code is in submissions/ and research/<investigation>/runs/. Per-run NVML logs, Modal stdout, and harness results are committed alongside each submission.

Summary

Three submissions out of 24 clear the 0.70 floor: the modded-nanogpt transformer baseline, and two LWTA drop-ins that replace GELU with group-wise winner-take-all. The LWTA win is a real but modest 10–11% energy reduction at slightly lower accuracy — mechanically, an MLP-activation change rather than a different learning algorithm.

Below the floor, the most efficient submission is PPM-C (0.6525 / 735 J) — a count-based context-tree algorithm running 70× cheaper than the baseline. The strongest sub-floor accuracy is Pointer-Sentinel (0.6712 / 20 kJ) at ~40% of the baseline's energy, with a 0.029 gap to the floor. Three sub-quadratic architectures (Mamba, Hyena, the full Schmidhuber chunker) train inside the budget but fail to complete byte-by-byte evaluation in pure PyTorch — the harness disqualifies on total wall-clock, so the inference substrate matters as much as the training substrate.

The headline research question — can a fundamentally different learning algorithm beat backprop on this task — is still open. PPM has hit its character-level ceiling at ~0.65. Forward-Forward's rule adds only ~0.06 over no learning at all. Pointer-Sentinel and Mamba are the two architectures closest to passing on the current evidence, and both look like they want one more iteration of the substrate (wider backbone or longer buffer for the former; CUDA scan kernel or windowed eval for the latter) before they can clear the floor.
