Headline result. The five most energy-efficient passing submissions on this branch are all classical-style count predictors: n-gram count tables built on the GPU, smoothed by Kneser-Ney or Witten-Bell, and (in two cases) context-mixed by a tiny MLP in the spirit of PAQ8. Every one of them clears the 0.70 val char-acc floor at under 4 kJ, while the cheapest passing neural-style submission on this branch (lwta_k4_alpha_065) lands at 13.2 kJ. Top entry subset_70_mkn uses 48.6× less energy than the modded-nanogpt baseline at a 0.034 acc deficit. The single-axis story of the benchmark — energy to reach the floor — favors the model class that pays nothing at predict time except a binary search and a fixed-size matmul.

#1 · subset_70_mkn — Modified Kneser-Ney, per-count discounts

1,065 J · 41.1 s · 0.7031 val acc · @gabrielnan

Build order-11 byte n-gram tables on 70 % of WikiText-103 using GPU sort+dedup; smooth with three per-count Chen-Goodman discounts (D₁, D₂, D₃) rather than a single scalar D.

Hover ratings: D₁ < D₂ < D₃ — singletons are penalized less than dense events relative to the canonical D = 0.5 baseline.

Why this wins on energy

There is no model in the gradient sense — no parameters that have to be updated by backprop. Training is a sort: pack each 11-byte window into two int64s, sort lexicographically on the GPU, run-length-encode to extract counts, then drop the leftmost byte and repeat to materialize orders 10, 9, …, 1. Total GPU work is O(N log N) sort time on N ≈ 380 M bytes — about 40 s of A100 time. The Chen-Goodman per-count discounts (D₁/D₂/D₃) are scalar per-order moments of the count-of-counts table; they cost milliseconds. The win over plain KN is small in absolute val-acc but real: D₁ ≈ 0.708 vs. the canonical 0.5 keeps more singletons in the tail, which the char-acc metric — biased toward short-range format/repetition prediction — rewards.

Key constants in the submission

MAX_ORDER = 11 · D₁..D₃ computed from per-order count-of-counts · chunk size 32 MB · 70 % subset = first 378,767,828 bytes · ~93 M unique 11-grams · final tables held as CPU numpy arrays for binary-search prediction.

#2 · gpu_ngram_w31_k11 — GPU-native order-11 Kneser-Ney

1,333 J · 33.6 s · 0.7050 val acc · @gabrielnan

Same backbone as method #1 but on the full training corpus with a single scalar discount D = 0.5; the interesting bit here is the dual-int64 packing trick that lets a stable two-pass GPU sort handle 11-byte keys with no hashing collisions.

Why dual-int64 not hash? Hashing risks collisions in a 25611 key space; packing is collision-free.

Why this wins on energy

The key insight is that you don't need a learned representation if your key space — 11 bytes of UTF-8 — fits in 88 bits. Two int64s exactly hold one window. PyTorch's torch.argsort then becomes the entire "training algorithm": one stable sort plus a run-length-encode is the count step. No custom CUDA kernels — pure torch ops. The chained step-down means you build the full orders 1–11 cascade without re-scanning the corpus once for each lower order; the (k−1)-byte representation just discards the leftmost byte from the int64 pair and resorts.

Why it scored 0.7050 and not higher

Scalar D = 0.5 is the classical-but-suboptimal Kneser-Ney discount. Method #1 keeps the same sort backbone and replaces this scalar with per-count discounts (D₁, D₂, D₃ ≈ 0.71, 1.13, 1.43) and loses 0.002 acc but recovers 268 J. So in this region of the energy axis, MKN's better smoothing barely matters; the cost of training is dominated by the sort, not the discount math.

#3 · paq8_gpu — PAQ8-style four-expert mixer

2,333 J · 68.2 s · 0.7248 val acc · @ab-10

Four parallel context models — KN backoff (orders 1–11), skip-1 sparse n-gram, case-folded order-7, and a longest-suffix match model — each emit a 256-bin distribution. A tiny 14→32→4 MLP mixer assigns per-position softmax weights using log-count, entropy, found-flag, and match-depth features. This is the highest-accuracy method in the top-5.

The four weights oscillate to illustrate context-dependent mixing — in code they are recomputed every byte from the feature vector.

What the mixer is actually doing

The 14-dim feature vector encodes uncertainty per expert: high entropy → trust the others; high logN → this context is well-attested; depth on the match model is the suffix length, which is the strongest single signal the system has (longer match → near-1 weight on the matched continuation byte). The mixer is trained for 1,500 Adam steps on 80,000 held-out positions from the last 2 MB of the corpus — total mixer compute is < 1 s of GPU time. The bulk of the 2,333 J budget is the order-1–11 KN table build, the same sort backbone as methods #1 and #2.

Why this is the top-5's accuracy leader (0.7248)

On char-acc, two of the experts are doing real work that pure KN cannot do: the match model catches verbatim repetition (page titles, section headings, "== References ==" markers — endemic to WikiText), and the case-folded order-7 predictor catches word stems independent of capitalization. Skip-1 is the weakest of the four but helps in syntactically rigid positions where the immediate previous character is the wrong cue (after a punctuation–space gap). Adding these costs +1,000 J over gpu_ngram_w31_k11 and buys +0.02 acc — a worthwhile trade on this benchmark.

#4 · gpu_ngram_o14_xorfix — sign-bit XOR for unsigned GPU sort

3,441 J · 97.6 s · 0.7184 val acc · @gabrielnan

Order-14 Kneser-Ney n-gram with the bug fix that mattered: at k ≥ 9 the packed int64 keys carry their MSB into the sign bit, and PyTorch's signed torch.sort scrambled the global lexicographic order. The fix is one XOR.

Without the XOR, the sister submission gpu_ngram_o14 wasted ~150 s on a CPU re-sort to repair the order before predict() could use binary search.

Why this is "the bug fix that mattered"

Sister submissions in this family hit a CPU re-sort step burning ~150 s of wall-clock — over half the entire 300 s budget — because the GPU's signed comparator had produced a key array that np.searchsorted could not use. XOR-ing the sign bit before the sort re-maps the int64 range so the negative half (0x80–0xFF leading bytes) becomes the high-positive half (0x8000000000000000…), and signed lex order becomes unsigned lex order "for free". Wall-clock drops from ~250 s to 97.6 s. Energy follows.

Order 14 vs. order 11

Why pay 3.4 kJ for k=14 when k=11 (method #2) costs 1.3 kJ and lands the same accuracy? Empirically, the answer is "it doesn't pay" on char-acc. The order-14 table is fatter (~50M unique 14-grams vs. ~10M unique 11-grams) and the extra long-range context boosts log-loss but char-acc moves only +0.013. So this entry is a Pareto-stuck design: the XOR fix saves it from DQ, but it's still dominated on the energy axis by the lower-order siblings.

#5 · paq_mixer_v3 — 11 independent experts, no backoff chain

3,582 J · 122.3 s · 0.7047 val acc · @gabrielnan

A PAQ-shaped mixer that throws away the chained backoff: each of the 11 byte n-gram orders is treated as an independent expert with Witten-Bell smoothing, and a 22→32→11 MLP learns soft weights from per-order [logN, entropy, found] features. Errors in one order can no longer cascade into others.

Mixer trains with cross-entropy on the true next byte: L = −log Σ_k softmax(w)_k · p_k(c*|h).

What this design buys

Decoupling the orders means the mixer can attribute high entropy to a single bad expert without polluting the others, where canonical KN backoff would have to walk all the way down. The cost is that you're always evaluating all 11 distributions at predict time; this is why the train wall-clock is 122 s vs. 33 s for the plain order-11 sibling — the extra work is the mixer-training step (1,500 Adam steps on 200K positions sampled from a held-out 2 MB slice).

Why it lands below paq8_gpu (0.7047 vs. 0.7248)

Heterogeneity beats parallelism here. paq8_gpu mixes four different kinds of context (regular KN, skip-1, case-fold, match) while v3 mixes eleven copies of the same idea at different orders. The match model on its own catches high-mass repetition that no order-k n-gram can; the case-fold model decouples capitalization. Stacking more orders of the same family has hit diminishing returns by k=8.

4 · Cross-cutting patterns

4.1 · The five winners share one tool: GPU sort as the training algorithm

All five use torch.argsort on packed byte windows as the workhorse step. None uses backprop on the n-gram tables themselves. The only learned-by-gradient component on the entire leaderboard is the ≤1,100-parameter mixer in methods #3 and #5 — and even those train for under 2 s of GPU time, well below the 300 s budget. Where the energy goes: GPU sort on N ≈ 400 M-byte sequences, repeated 11–14 times for the order cascade. Where it does not go: anywhere near a transformer's parameter gradient buffer.

The smoothing choice is overshadowed by the size of the count tables you need to build. 0.70 is "cheap" in this design space; pushing toward 0.74 (where the modded-nanogpt baseline sits) is what costs you.

4.3 · Mixing heterogeneous experts beats mixing more-of-the-same

paq8_gpu (4 different experts) beats paq_mixer_v3 (11 same-family experts) by +0.02 val acc at lower energy. The heterogeneity supplies signal the n-gram cascade cannot — primarily the match model, which exploits WikiText's verbatim reuse of section markers, page titles, and citation templates. This generalizes a long-known PAQ-family lesson: the lift comes from diversity of contexts, not diversity of smoothing.

4.4 · The benchmark's char-acc metric rewards format prediction

All five winners cluster at 0.700–0.725 val acc. The neural baseline (modded-nanogpt) reaches 0.7374 at ~50× the energy. The gap is small because the char-acc metric, computed on the first 60K chars of validation, weights heavily on short-range format/repetition (whitespace, section markers, capitalization patterns). A model that nails these wins on char-acc without ever needing the long-range semantic abstractions that justify deep nets. If the benchmark were log-loss or BPB, the picture would be very different.
